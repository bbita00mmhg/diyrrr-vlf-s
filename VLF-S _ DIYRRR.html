Very Low Frequency-S

This tutorial will guide you through building an extremely low-frequency (VLF) receiver based on Techlib’s Peanut Butter design. This circuit allows you to pick up natural signals such as Schumann resonances, atmospheric emissions, and other electromagnetic waves present in the environment.

Wise advice

2.1. Preparing for soldering

1.Heat the soldering iron: Plug it in and wait a few minutes until it reaches the right temperature. If the solder doesn’t melt easily, it’s not hot enough yet.

2.Prepare the connections: Bend the component leads to fit the perforated board and clean the metal surfaces with isopropyl alcohol to remove dirt.
3.Secure the components: Use helping hands or tape to hold them in place while soldering.

Click to enlarge image

2.2. Soldering Order

1. Solder the smallest components first: Begin with resistors, small capacitors, and diodes. These are easier to place and won’t move when soldering larger parts later.

2. Move on to integrated circuits (ICs): If using ICs, consider using a socket instead of soldering them directly to avoid heat damage.
3. Solder connectors and switches: These parts usually need extra mechanical stability, so ensure strong joints.
4. Attach larger components: Inductors, large capacitors, and transformers should be soldered last since they may require more heat.
5. Solder the antenna wire: Finally, connect the enamel-coated copper wire to the appropriate input, ensuring a good connection for better signal reception.

2.3. Final Steps

1. Inspect the solder joints: They should be shiny, smooth, and without bridges (accidental connections).

2. Trim excess leads with wire cutters.
3. Clean the board with alcohol to remove flux residues.
Test continuity with a multimeter to ensure there are no short circuits.
5. Solder the antenna wire: Finally, connect the enamel-coated copper wire to the appropriate input, ensuring a good connection for better signal reception.

3 Antenna

3.1. Preparing the biomaterial

1. Measure ingredients: Use a ratio of 2g agar-agar per 100ml distilled water and add 10-20% rock powder for conductivity.

2. Heat the mixture: In a small pot, bring the water to a light boil and gradually add the agar-agar while stirring continuously.

3. Incorporate the rock powder: Slowly sprinkle it into the mixture, ensuring an even distribution. Stir until fully dissolved.

4. Pour and shape: Transfer the mixture onto a mold or a flat surface, spreading it to a thin, uniform layer (about 1-3mm thick).

5. Let it solidify: Allow it to cool at room temperature for at least 30 minutes or until firm.

3.2. Conductive Stitching

1. Prepare the silver thread: Cut a length based on your antenna design (loop, spiral, or straight-line configurations).


2. Sew the pattern: Use a needle to stitch the silver thread onto the biomaterial, ensuring good contact with the surface.


3. Secure connections: If needed, use a small amount of conductive glue or a soldering iron (at very low heat) to reinforce key junctions.


4. Attach to the circuit: Connect the stitched thread to the receiver’s input, ensuring a stable link for signal transmission.

3.3. Testing & Optimization

1. Check conductivity using a multimeter.

2. Experiment with different rock powders to enhance reception.

3. Adjust the stitching pattern for optimal signal capture.
